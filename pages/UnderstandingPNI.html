Understanding PNI

Anatomy of a Nerve

What is a nerve?

Nerves help us sense and react to the world around us. We have nerves throughout our body, including our skin and organs, that communicate with our brain. Nerves act like telephone wires that carry signals to communicate between the body and the brain.

There are two main types of nerves. Sensory nerves help you feel your environment, such as when you touch something hot like a stovetop. Sensory nerves will send that information to your brain, allowing you to register it as pain.

Motor nerves can activate your muscles, sometimes to respond to a sensation like pain. They will carry signals from your brain that tell your muscles to stop, continue, or start moving.

There are several layers of organization within a nerve. These layers of connective tissue are important because they help doctors classify the severity of nerve injury.

Each nerve is surrounded by a layer of connective tissue called the epineurium. Within the nerve, you will find fat, blood vessels, and smaller nerve bundles called fascicles.

Each fascicle is wrapped in their own protective layer of connective tissue called the perineurium. A fascicle is made up of bundles of nerve cells, called neurons.

Neurons are the basic building blocks of the nervous system. Each neuron is wrapped in a layer of connective tissue called the endoneurium.

Nerve injuries are classified as more severe when more layers of the nerve are damaged.

The Peripheral Nervous System

What is the peripheral nervous system?

Together, nerves, the spinal cord, and your brain make up the nervous system. The nervous system can be divided into two parts:

  • The central nervous system includes your brain and spinal cord. It sends signals to the rest of the body.
  • The peripheral nervous system includes all the nerves outside of the central nervous system. It sends signals to the spinal cord and the brain.

When peripheral nerve injury occurs, this means nerves in the peripheral nervous system have been damaged.

What is peripheral nerve injury?

Peripheral nerve injury (PNI) can affect your muscles or ability to feel sensations. Some common symptoms of PNI are:

  • muscle weakness,
  • tingling/numbness,
  • or pain.

Every patient will have the different symptoms because each injury is different. How serious the injury is, where it happens, and how much of the nerve is damaged can change how a person's muscles or senses are affected.

Classifying an Injury

How can peripheral nerve injury happen?

Compression/Crush Injury

Compression: The nerve is compressed or squeezed over time.

Crush: A more severe form of compression that happens suddenly due to severe, blunt force trauma.

Most injury will result in mild, reversible injury.

Traction Injury

The nerve is stretched or pulled, which can lead to the nerve being torn partially or completely.

Injuries can result in a range of severity depending on the extent of stretching.

Laceration/Transction Injury

Laceration: Partial or complete cutting of the nerve, usually from a sharp object.

Transection: The nerve is completely cut and can only be repaired through surgery.


What are the categories of PNI?

Peripheral nerve injury can be categorized by the severity of the injury and which nerve structures are damaged.

Neuropraxia - Grade 1 Injury

Structural changes: The myelin sheath is damaged, but the axon and all of the surrounding connective tissues are healthy.

Symptoms: Pain, muscle weakness, or numbness.

Outcomes: Usually complete recovery is possible.

Axonotmesis - Grade 2, 3, or 4 Injury

Structural changes: The myelin sheath AND axons are damaged. The surrounding connective tissues may be damaged to varying degrees.

  • Grade 2: None of the connective tissues are damaged.
  • Grade 3: The endoneurium is also damaged.
  • Grade 4: The endoneurium and perineurium are damaged.

Symptoms: Pain, different amounts of motor and/or sensory loss depending on severity of injury.

Outcomes: Recovery is possible, but may take different amounts of time depending on the amount of damage. The higher the grade, the less likely it is for the nerve to completely heal.

Neurotmesis - Grade 5 Injury

Structural changes: The nerve is fully cut, including through the myelin sheath, axons, and all layers of the connective tissue.

Symptoms: Severe pain, complete motor and/or sensory loss.

Outcomes: Axons cannot grow back because the nerve is fully cut or scar tissue blocks the way. Surgery is needed for any sort of recovery to occur.

Arm Injuries

Nerves in the Arm

How are nerves in the arm organized?

On both sides of the body, a complex network of nerves called the brachial plexus is located near the collarbone. The brachial plexus connects nerves from the spinal cord to the nerves in the arm.

The nerves from the brachial plexus are responsible for arm sensation and movement.

Because nerve connections are continuous from your spinal cord to your fingers, any injury that breaks or disrupts nerve connections will impact nerve function.

Starting from the neck and moving towards your hand, the brachial plexus is organized into sections called nerve roots, trunks, divisions, cords, and terminal branches.

Like branches on a tree, the nerves of the brachial plexus split and recombine into different sections until becoming the terminal nerves, which are the last major nerve branches.

Roots are the initial nerves that come off the spinal cord.The roots join to form trunks which are subdivided into the:

  • upper trunk,
  • middle trunk,
  • and lower trunk.

Each trunk splits into anterior (front) and posterior (back) divisions that go to the front and back of the arm respectively. There is an anterior and posterior division for the upper, middle, and lower trunk.

Further down, the divisions recombine to form three new sections called cords. These subdivide into the:

  • posterior cord,
  • lateral cord,
  • and medial cord.

The brachial plexus ends in five major nerves called the terminal nervesthat control different parts of the arm. These are the:

  • musculocutaneous nerve
  • axillary nerve
  • median nerve
  • radial nerve
  • ulnar nerve.

What function does each nerve have?

Most nerves will control a mix of sensation and muscle movement. Nerves that control sensation will carry signals from the skin to the brain, while nerves that control movement will carry signals from the brain to the muscles.

Types of Injuries

What happens when nerves in the arm are damaged?

Any nerve or combination of nerves may be damaged in peripheral nerve injury. Terminal nerves can be individually damaged and also require surgical treatment. Generally, when the injury is closer to the spinal cord, more of the arm will be affected.

If you feel pain, tingling, or numbness in your arm, this means that nerves involved in sensation are injured.

If your arm feels weaker or muscles look smaller, this means that nerves involved in movement are injured.

Brachial Plexus Injury

Brachial plexus injury can be more severe and complex because the brachial plexus is closer to the spinal cord. This means that many arm functions or sensations may be impacted.

Location

Because the brachial plexus is close to the collarbone, brachial plexus injury can be described based whether they occur above, behind, or below the collarbone.

  • Above the collarbone or supraclavicular (injury to the root & trunk)
  • Behind the collarbone or retroclavicular (divisions)
  • Below the collarbone or infraclavicular (injury to the cords & terminal
  • nerves)

Area of Brachial Plexus

Brachial plexus injuries can also be categorized by whether the upper, middle, or lower trunk is involved.

Upper Trunk Injury: Nerves that make up the upper trunk send signals to the upper area of the arm and shoulder.

  • Sensory symptoms: Upper trunk injury can cause loss of feeling or numbness in the shoulder, outside of arm, and thumb.
  • Motor symptoms: You may also be unable to lift your arm at the shoulder, bend your arm at the elbow, or feel like your arm is weak in general. In extreme cases, paralysis of shoulder muscles and biceps can occur.

Middle Trunk Injury: Injury to the middle trunk can affect some wrist and forearm muscles. However, the middle trunk is rarely injured on its own because of its location. When it is injured, it is usually along with other parts of the brachial plexus.

Lower Trunk Injury: When the lower trunk alone is injured, strength in the shoulder and elbow are usually preserved, but hand function is affected.

  • Your hand may be in a claw position at rest with difficulties performing fine motor tasks.
  • You may also experience hand numbness in ring finger and small finger.

Pan Plexus Injury: In a pan-plexus injury, all three trunks are damaged, leading to complete paralysis of the arm and hand.

Keep in mind that your symptoms might not match these descriptions exactly. It's important to share all the symptoms you're experiencing with your doctor so they can give you the most accurate diagnosis and care.
