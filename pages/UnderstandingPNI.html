Understanding PNI

Anatomy of a Nerve

What is a nerve?

Nerves help us sense and react to the world around us. We have nerves throughout our body, including our skin and organs, that communicate with our brain. Nerves act like telephone wires that carry signals to communicate between the body and the brain.

There are two main types of nerves. Sensory nerves help you feel your environment, such as when you touch something hot like a stovetop. Sensory nerves will send that information to your brain, allowing you to register it as pain.

Motor nerves can activate your muscles, sometimes to respond to a sensation like pain. They will carry signals from your brain that tell your muscles to stop, continue, or start moving.

What is the structure of a nerve?

There are several layers of organization within a nerve. These layers of connective tissue are important because they help doctors classify the severity of nerve injury.

Each nerve is surrounded by a layer of connective tissue called the epineurium. Within the nerve, you will find fat, blood vessels, and smaller nerve bundles called fascicles.

Each fascicle is wrapped in their own protective layer of connective tissue called the perineurium. A fascicle is made up of bundles of nerve cells, called neurons.

Neurons are the basic building blocks of the nervous system. Each neuron is wrapped in a layer of connective tissue called the endoneurium.

Nerve injuries are classified as more severe when more layers of the nerve are damaged.

The Peripheral Nervous System

What is the peripheral nervous system?

Together, nerves, the spinal cord, and your brain make up the nervous system. The nervous system can be divided into two parts:

  • The central nervous system includes your brain and spinal cord. It sends signals to the rest of the body.
  • The peripheral nervous system includes all the nerves outside of the central nervous system. It sends signals to the spinal cord and the brain.

When peripheral nerve injury occurs, this means nerves in the peripheral nervous system have been damaged.

What is peripheral nerve injury?

Peripheral nerve injury (PNI) can affect your muscles or ability to feel sensations. Some common symptoms of PNI are:

  • muscle weakness,
  • tingling/numbness,
  • or pain.

Every patient will have the different symptoms because each injury is different. How serious the injury is, where it happens, and how much of the nerve is damaged can change how a person's muscles or senses are affected.

Classifying an Injury

How can peripheral nerve injury happen?

Compression/Crush Injury

Compression: The nerve is compressed or squeezed over time.

Crush: A more severe form of compression that happens suddenly due to severe, blunt force trauma.

Most injury will result in mild, reversible injury.

Traction Injury

The nerve is stretched or pulled, which can lead to the nerve being torn partially or completely.

Injuries can result in a range of severity depending on the extent of stretching.

Laceration/Transction Injury

Laceration: Partial or complete cutting of the nerve, usually from a sharp object.

Transection: The nerve is completely cut and can only be repaired through surgery.

What are the categories of PNI?

Peripheral nerve injury can be categorized by the severity of the injury and which nerve structures are damaged.

Neuropraxia - Grade 1 Injury

Structural changes: The myelin sheath is damaged, but the axon and all of the surrounding connective tissues are intact.

Symptoms: Pain, muscle weakness, or numbness.

Outcomes: Usually complete recovery is possible.

Axonotmesis - Grade 2, 3, or 4 Injury

Structural changes: The myelin sheath and axons are damaged, but all of the surrounding connective tissues are intact or damaged to varying degrees.

  • Grade 2: None of the connective tissues are damaged.
  • Grade 3: In addition to the myelin sheath and axons, the endoneurium is damaged.
  • Grade 4: In addition to the myelin sheath and axons, the endoneurium and perineurium is damaged.

Symptoms: Pain, different amounts of motor and/or sensory loss depending on severity of injury.

Outcomes: Recovery is possible, but may take different amounts of time depending on the amount of damage. The higher the grade, the less likely it is for the nerve to completely heal.

Neurotmesis - Grade 5 Injury

Structural changes: The nerve is fully cut, including through the myelin sheath, axons, and all layers of the connective tissue.

Symptoms: Severe pain, complete motor and/or sensory loss.

Outcomes: Axons cannot grow back because the nerve is fully cut or scar tissue blocks the way. Surgery is needed for any sort of recovery to occur.
