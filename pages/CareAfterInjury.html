Care After Injury

After getting diagnosed, your care team will recommend a specific treatment plan. It's important to know that the recovery process after peripheral nerve injury will look different for everyone. The amount of time needed for recovery to happen and the amount of recovery that is possible will be different.

It’s important to discuss your goals for recovery with your care team. Based on your conversation, they will be able to present you with the treatment options that are the best fit.

Lots of things are uncertain during this time, and it can feel frustrating to not know what is going to happen in the future. While your care team may not have all the answers, they are here to listen to your concerns and answer any questions you may have.

Treatment Options

Non-Surgical Treatment

Spontaneous Recovery

Sometimes, nerves can naturally regrow after being injured. This is called spontaneous recovery. Axons of neurons can regenerate at a rate of about 1mm/day if they are not completely cut or seriously injured.

But, this is not possible with all nerve injuries. Recovery will depend on the following factors:

  • Severity of injury:

    Severe injuries cause more damage to the layers of the nerve. This makes it harder for spontaneous recovery to occur.

  • Location of injury:

    Nerves need to regrow from the injury all the way back to the muscle or skin. The further the site of injury is from the end target, the longer it will take for the nerve to regrow.

  • Presence of scar tissue:

    Scar tissue can block the path of the growing nerve. This slows growth and makes it less likely that the nerve will reach its end target.

  • Aging:

    As we get older, our body’s natural ability to heal and recover from injury is slowed. This may impact the speed of spontaneous recovery of the nerve.

Generally, spontaneous recovery is preferred over surgery and leads to better recovery. But, if spontaneous recovery does not happen, it is best for surgery to occur within 3-6 months of injury for better recovery.

During this period of time, things might feel at a standstill. Your healthcare team will carefully monitor the injury during follow-up appointments to determine whether spontaneous recovery is occurring, or if surgical treatment is likely to provide better results.

Do I have to get surgery?

If surgery is not an option, other treatments might help with managing the nerve injury.

  • This can include using tools like slings or splints, to hold the injured area still and stop unwanted movement.

  • Physical therapy can gently exercise the area to keep joints and muscles active.

  • Electrical stimulation may also help with any pain or discomfort.

Remember to always consult your care team for the best option for your treatment.

Surgical Treatment

When should I get surgery?

Surgery may be a good option if there is no evidence of spontaneous recovery within 6 months of injury. The optimal time for surgery is between 3-6 months post injury. The type of injury you have may also affect the timing of surgery.

What are my surgical options?

Primary Nerve Repair/Neurorraphy

In primary nerve repair, the two ends of the nerves are aligned and sutured back together through the epineurium.

The recovery for this surgery are the best when done as soon as possible.

Nerve Graft

Nerve grafts are done when primary nerve repair can’t be done, such as if the two ends of the nerve are too far apart to join back together.

During a nerve graft surgery, the injured part of the nerve is replaced with a section of nerve taken from another part of the body.

This nerve is usually connected to the same muscle as many other nerves so that even if it is removed, the muscle still works.

The section of the nerve is used to connect the two ends of the injured nerve together.

Nerve Transfer

During a nerve transfer, doctors try to connect a nearby working nerve to the muscle that isn't working. This working nerve may be less important or innervate a muscle that already has nerve connections. The working nerve is attached to the injured end that is still connected to the target muscle.

This surgery guides the neurons from the working nerve to travel into the injured nerve to reinnervate the muscle.

Rehabilitation

After Surgery

Immobilization and rest

After surgery, make sure to follow-up with your surgeon or care team.

Immediately after surgery, the injured limb will likely be bandaged with a dressing and held in place with a splint, sling, or shoulder immobilizer. This is to stop unwanted movement and allow the nerve to heal.

Exercise and movement

To prevent muscle loss and stiff joints, you may be taught some range of motion exercises. Consistently doing these exercises will help you regain some range of motion.

These exercises can also help reduce swelling and pain. They can be adjusted over time to help strengthen your muscles as you heal.

Monitoring your progress

Your care team will also check our ability to sense and use your muscle during your appointments to see whether the nerve is healing.

To help them better understand how the injury might still be affecting you day-to day, you may be asked to complete questionnaires about your injury during your visit.

Restoring Movement and Sensation

After surgeries like nerve grafts or nerve transfers, the connection between the nerves and muscles need to be reinforced. The muscle that was connected to the damaged nerve is now receiving signals from a different nerve.

Because of this swap, we will need to retrain our brain to create a new connection between the nerve and the muscle. Exercises that help with this are known as sensorimotor re-education.

This can include:

  • movement exercises that work the new muscle,
  • practicing being able to identify where you are being touched,
  • and feeling different surfaces or objects to practice telling the difference between textures.

Biofeedback

Electrical stimulation through a process known as surface EMG biofeedback can also help you regain muscle strength and control.

  1. Small electrodes are placed on the weakened/injured muscles. These electrodes can detect the signals produced when the muscles activate.

  2. When you try to use the muscle, these signals are detected and converted into a visual or audio indicator, like a graph, or a beep.

    After injury when the muscle is weak, you may not be able to feel it working. However this machine is able to pick up signals that show you that muscle is still being activated.

  3. Seeing the feedback in real-time can help you practice reactivating the correct muscle until you can actually feel it working.

This also helps strengthen the connections between the new nerve and muscle, and rewire your brain into knowing how to contract the muscle with the new nerve.
