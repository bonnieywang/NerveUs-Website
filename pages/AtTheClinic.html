At the Clinic

The peripheral nerve injury clinic brings together a team of healthcare specialists who are typically seen at different stages of treatment. By having them all in one place, your wait time is reduced and your care team stays fully up-to-date on your injury and treatment plan.

At your appointment, you may see a few of these specialists depending on your specific concerns and where you are in your recovery. You might also be asked to complete diagnostic tests or functional assessments to better understand your injury, functional changes, or monitor your recovery progress.

Your Care Team

Your care team is made up of healthcare professionals with a wide range of skill sets, and expertise. This section provides information on:

  • What they do.
  • What kind of information they can provide you with.

Physiatrists

(fiz-ay-uh-trists)

Physiatrists are doctors that focus on how the body moves. They pay attention to how your body works, not just where it hurts. They are experts in peripheral nerve injury and can diagnose and treat pain or movement problems.

Your physiatrist may check how well you can move by asking you to do simple tasks. They may use electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction tests to take a closer look at your nerves and muscles.

Surgeons

Once you've been diagnosed, a surgeon may talk to you if you need surgery. Depending on where and how serious the nerve injury is, surgery can help reduce pain or improve function. After surgery, surgeons may also follow-up with you to see how youâ€™re recovering and if other treatments are needed.

Plastic surgeons, neurosurgeons, and orthopedic surgeons are skilled in fixing nerves or the tissue around them.

Physical Therapists

A physical therapist (PT) will help reduce pain and improve your ability to move . They might show you exercises or stretches to help you regain movement in your arm or leg. They can also help create a recovery plan to prevent future injury.

Occupational Therapists

An occupational therapist (OT) will help you get back to everyday activities, like getting dressed or doing school or work tasks. If daily activities feel harder after your injury, they can help you find new ways to do them.

They can also support you withdifficult emotions difficult emotions during recovery, especially if the injury has changed your life.

OTs may also suggest tools like braces or splints to support your injured arm or leg. These can help make daily tasks easier while keeping you safe.

Diagnostic Testing

Diagnostic tests help doctors understand the cause of pain or reduced function. They can also show how serious the nerve injury is. This information helps them choose the best treatment plan for you.

Medical History and Physical Exam

Before conducting any tests, the doctor will ask you questions about your injury. They will also look at your medical history to see if there are any health problems that could impact treatment and recovery.

The doctor will also check the injured area. You might be asked to do some simple movements to figure out which muscles and nerves have been affected.

Medical Imaging

To help understand what structures are affected, you may be asked to do some imaging exams. These can include:

  • X-rays
  • CT scans
  • MRIs
  • Ultrasounds

X-rays and CT scans both use a type of radiation that has a small risk of harming living tissue. This risk will increase with more exposures to radiation, however, the risk of developing cancer from this exposure is quite low. X-rays and CT scans are incredibly helpful tools for doctors to diagnose conditions to get you the care you need.

X-ray

X-rays show images of structures inside the body, such as your bones and organs.

During an x-ray, radiation is passed through your body. Tissues that absorb x-rays easily, such as bone, will appear whiter on the x-ray. The final image is created from the different amounts of radiation that reach the detector.

X-rays can help doctors see major bone injuries that might be around the nerve.

CT Scan

CT scans use stacked x-rays to create detailed, cross-sectional images of the body. They are also good at showing hard structures such as bone, but are more detailed than x-rays.

During a CT scan, you will lie on a bed that moves through a CT machine. As you move through the machine, x-rays will pass through your body. With new machines, CT scans can be done quite quickly, taking less than 15 minutes.

Doctors use CT scans to see bone injuries in detail and get a better look at the structures around the nerve.

MRI

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) provides detailed cross-sectional images of nerves and surrounding tissue. Unlike CT scans, MRIs are good at getting detailed pictures of bone AND soft tissues, such as nerves, tendons, or blood vessels.

Instead of radiation, MRIs use radio waves and magnetic fields to create cross-sectional images of your body.

During an MRI, you will lay on a bed that moves through an MRI machine. This machine acts like a giant magnet that sends radio waves through your body. This scan can take up to an hour to complete, depending on how much of your body needs to be scanned.

Doctors use MRIs to get more detailed images of nerves and the site of injury.

Ultrasound

Ultrasounds help doctors see nerves and the surrounding area in real-time and during movement.

During an ultrasound, a device called a transducer is placed over the skin. The transducer passes sound waves through the body. Different tissues will reflect the sound waves in different ways to show the location and shape of bones, muscles, nerves, and more.

There are no known risks for getting an ultrasound, but sound waves donâ€™t travel as well through the body, so the image may not be as clear.

Nerve Tests

Electrical signals sent by motor and sensory nerves are called action potentials. Normal action potentials have a specific wave appearance that can change with injury.

Needle Electromyography (EMG)

Electromyography (pronounced: uh-leck-troh-migh-OG-ruh-fee) is a nerve test that measures the electrical activity of muscles and nerves.

Doctors can look at the appearance of action potentials or how often they occur. If something looks unusual, it might mean thereâ€™s a problem with the connection between the nerve and muscle.

Needle electrodes are special, small needles that measure electrical signals. During needle electromyography, the needle electrode is inserted through the skin. You may be asked to stay still or gently contract your muscles.

Nerve Conduction Study

A nerve conduction study measures the speed and strength of action potentials passing in a nerve.

Surface electrodes are placed on your skin at different points. A brief, mild electric pulse will be sent through one electrode to the other. The size and speed of the signal can help identify the severity and type of nerve damage that has occurred.
